\section{Introduction} 

\subsection{Background}
The rapid growth of urban populations and the increasing number of motor vehicles have placed significant pressure on road infrastructure and traffic management systems. One of the most critical challenges in modern transportation is the high frequency of traffic light violations, which remain a leading cause of urban road accidents and congestion. Conventional methods of traffic enforcement, such as manual monitoring by officers or basic static camera systems, are often inefficient, labor-intensive, and prone to human error, making 24/7 surveillance difficult to maintain.

To address these challenges, we are trying to automate the surveillance process using a video from CCTV by utilizing a method in Computer Vision and Artificial Intelligence. By implementing real-time object detection and tracking, specifically through models like YOLOv8 for vehicle identification and DeepSORT for trajectory tracking, it is now possible to automate the monitoring of traffic flow. Furthermore, integrating specialized color analysis using the HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) model allows for high-precision detection of traffic light states under varying environmental lighting conditions.

This project proposes an automated traffic violation detection system that synchronizes vehicle tracking with traffic signal states. By digitizing the enforcement process, this system aims to enhance road safety and reduce the workload on traffic authorities.
\subsection{Problem Statement}
Traffic signal violations at Simpang Gedongan, Sleman, pose significant risks to road safety, yet conventional manual surveillance remains inefficient and prone to human error. Developing an automated detection system presents technical challenges, particularly in maintaining high accuracy for diverse vehicle types in dense traffic and ensuring robust traffic light recognition under varying lighting conditions. This study addresses these issues by implementing an integrated framework that utilizes YOLOv8 for high precision vehicle detection, DeepSORT for stable trajectory tracking, and HSV based segmentation for reliable signal state identification. By synchronizing these components, the proposed system aims to provide an efficient solution for automated traffic enforcement at complex urban intersections.

\subsection{HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) Color Model}
In the field of computer vision, selecting an appropriate color space is critical for the accuracy of object detection under varying environmental conditions. The HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) color model is a cylindrical representation of colors that more closely aligns with human visual perception compared to the standard RGB model. While RGB defines colors as a combination of primary light sources, it is highly sensitive to changes in brightness, shadows, and external lighting.The HSV model decouples color information from intensity, using three distinct components:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Hue ($H$): Represents the dominant wavelength or the actual "color" (e.g., Red, Yellow, Green) expressed in degrees from $0^{\circ}$ to $360^{\circ}$.
    \item Saturation ($S$): Indicates the vibrancy or purity of the color, ranging from $0$ to $100\%$.
    \item Value ($V$): Represents the brightness or luminance of the color.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Proposed Method} 
This study proposes a computer vision–based approach for detecting traffic signal violations using CCTV at Simpang Gedongan Sleman footage from a static camera. The system is designed as a multi-stage pipeline consisting of preprocessing, vehicle detection, object tracking, traffic light recognition, and violation analysis (Fig.~\ref{fig:proposed_method}). To improve efficiency and reduce noise, a polygon-based Region of Interest (ROI) is defined to limit the analysis to the roadway area, excluding irrelevant regions such as sidewalks and surrounding infrastructure.

Within this ROI, vehicle detection is performed using YOLOv8, focusing only on vehicles located inside the polygonal area. The detected vehicles are then processed using an object tracking method to assign unique identities and maintain their trajectories across consecutive frames. This tracking process enables consistent monitoring of each vehicle’s movement over time, which is essential for determining whether a vehicle has crossed a predefined boundary.

In parallel, the system identifies the traffic light state using a color-based approach, which is used as a condition in the violation detection process. A virtual stop line is defined within the ROI to represent the legal stopping boundary. A violation is detected when a tracked vehicle crosses this line while the traffic light is in a red state. The system then generates an output in the form of visual annotations and alert messages, providing a structured and automated method for traffic violation detection.

\subsection{Preprocessing}
Before being processed by the detection and tracking modules, the input frames undergo several preprocessing steps to improve efficiency and ensure consistency. First, each frame is resized to a fixed resolution of 768 × 768 pixels to match the input requirements of the detection model. This step also helps maintain a balance between computational cost and detection accuracy, particularly for detecting small objects such as motorcycles in CCTV footage.

Next, a Region of Interest (ROI) is defined in the form of a polygon to restrict the analysis to the roadway area. By applying this ROI, the system ignores irrelevant regions such as sidewalks, buildings, and other background elements, thereby reducing noise and improving detection performance. All subsequent processes, including vehicle detection and tracking, are performed only within this polygonal region.

\subsection{Traffic Light Detection using HSV}
By utilizing the HSV color space, the proposed system can isolate the Hue component to identify traffic light states. This provides the algorithm with significant robustness against illumination variance, ensuring that the system can accurately distinguish between "Red" and "Green" signals regardless of whether the footage is captured during daylight or nighttime conditions.

\subsection{Violation Detection}
The violation detection process is conducted by integrating vehicle trajectory information with the detected traffic light state (Fig.~\ref{fig:violation_detection}). A virtual boundary, referred to as the stop line, is defined within the Region of Interest (ROI) to represent the legal stopping limit at the intersection. Each detected vehicle is assigned a unique identity through the tracking module, enabling continuous monitoring of its movement across frames. By analyzing the position of each tracked vehicle relative to the stop line, the system determines whether the vehicle remains within the allowed area or crosses the predefined boundary.

At the same time, the traffic light state is identified using HSV-based color detection within a predefined traffic light region. A violation is defined as the event where a tracked vehicle crosses the stop line while the traffic light is in a red state. When this condition is met, the system flags the vehicle as a violator and generates visual outputs, such as bounding boxes, object IDs, and alert indicators, to clearly represent the detected violation.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
To assess the performance of the proposed traffic violation detection system, several standard statistical metrics will be utilized. These metrics provide a quantitative measure of how well the YOLOv8 and DEEPSORT model detects objects.
\subsubsection{Accuracy}
The ratio of total correct predictions to the total number of test cases.
$$Accuracy = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}$$
\subsubsection{Precision}
Measures the proportion of predicted violations that were actually correct.
$$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}$$
\subsubsection{Recall}
Measures the system's ability to capture all actual violations.
$$Recall = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}$$
\subsubsection{F1-Score}
The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall.
$$F1 = 2 \times \frac{Precision \times Recall}{Precision + Recall}$$

\section{Introduction} 

\subsection{Background}
The rapid growth of urban populations and the increasing number of motor vehicles have placed significant pressure on road infrastructure and traffic management systems. One of the most critical challenges in modern transportation is the high frequency of traffic light violations, which remain a leading cause of urban road accidents and congestion. Conventional methods of traffic enforcement, such as manual monitoring by officers or basic static camera systems, are often inefficient, labor-intensive, and prone to human error, making 24/7 surveillance difficult to maintain.

To address these challenges, we are trying to automate the surveillance process using a video from CCTV by utilizing a method in Computer Vision and Artificial Intelligence. By implementing real-time object detection and tracking, specifically through models like YOLOv8 for vehicle identification and DeepSORT for trajectory tracking, it is now possible to automate the monitoring of traffic flow. Furthermore, integrating specialized color analysis using the HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) model allows for high-precision detection of traffic light states under varying environmental lighting conditions.

This project proposes an automated traffic violation detection system that synchronizes vehicle tracking with traffic signal states. By digitizing the enforcement process, this system aims to enhance road safety and reduce the workload on traffic authorities.
\subsection{Problem Statement}
Traffic signal violations at Simpang Gedongan, Sleman, pose significant risks to road safety, yet conventional manual surveillance remains inefficient and prone to human error. Developing an automated detection system presents technical challenges, particularly in maintaining high accuracy for diverse vehicle types in dense traffic and ensuring robust traffic light recognition under varying lighting conditions. 

This study addresses these issues by implementing an integrated framework that utilizes YOLOv8 for high precision vehicle detection, DeepSORT for stable trajectory tracking, and HSV based segmentation for reliable signal state identification. By synchronizing these components, the proposed system aims to provide an efficient solution for automated traffic enforcement at complex urban intersections.
\subsection{Objective}
The primary objective of this study is to develop a reliable and computationally efficient automated system for real-time traffic signal violation detection. To achieve this, the research aims to implement a vehicle detection module using HOG-SVM that maintains high accuracy without requiring excessive hardware resources. This will be integrated with a robust HSV-based segmentation method to identify traffic light states under varying environmental lighting conditions. Furthermore, the system will utilize the SORT algorithm to maintain consistent vehicle trajectories and unique object identities across video frames. Finally, the study focuses on building a synchronized violation logic that evaluates vehicle movement against traffic light signals, ultimately testing the integrated system’s overall performance in terms of detection accuracy and real-time processing speed.
\subsection{Scope}
This study is limited to traffic signal violation detection using static CCTV footage at Simpang Gedongan, Sleman, with a resolution of pixels. The system utilizes YOLOv8 for vehicle detection and DeepSORT for trajectory tracking within a predefined polygonal Region of Interest (ROI). Traffic light identification is restricted to HSV based color segmentation under clear weather and stable lighting conditions. The analysis focuses on common motor vehicles, such as cars and motorcycles, traversing the intersection from a single camera viewpoint.

\subsection{HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) Color Model}
In the field of computer vision, selecting an appropriate color space is critical for the accuracy of object detection under varying environmental conditions. The HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) color model is a cylindrical representation of colors that more closely aligns with human visual perception compared to the standard RGB model. While RGB defines colors as a combination of primary light sources, it is highly sensitive to changes in brightness, shadows, and external lighting.The HSV model decouples color information from intensity, using three distinct components:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Hue ($H$): Represents the dominant wavelength or the actual "color" (e.g., Red, Yellow, Green) expressed in degrees from $0^{\circ}$ to $360^{\circ}$.
    \item Saturation ($S$): Indicates the vibrancy or purity of the color, ranging from $0$ to $100\%$.
    \item Value ($V$): Represents the brightness or luminance of the color.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{You Only Look Once (YOLOv8)}
YOLOv8 represents a significant advancement, offering an optimal trade off between computational overhead and detection precision. Diverging from previous iterations, YOLOv8 adopts an anchor free detection paradigm, which mitigates model complexity and enhances generalization across multi scale object distributions. The architecture integrates a decoupled head design to process classification and localization tasks through independent branches, thereby facilitating more precise bounding box regression. 

The implementation of an optimized backbone and neck architecture facilitates superior feature extraction and gradient flow. These architectural enhancements render YOLOv8 highly effective for real time traffic surveillance at congested intersections, such as Simpang Gedongan, where diverse vehicle classes must be identified under dynamic conditions.
\subsection{Deep Simple Online and Realtime Tracking (DeepSORT)}
DeepSORT is an upgraded version of the SORT (Simple Online and Real-time Tracking) algorithm. Its main advantage is the ability to "remember" an object's visual features, which prevents the system from losing track or switching IDs when an object is briefly blocked or overlaps with another.

The technical workflow of DeepSORT is broken down into four main stages:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The system predicts where an object is headed using a Kalman Filter. It looks at the previous position and velocity to create an "educated guess" for the next frame. The state of each track is represented by an eight-dimensional vector:
    \begin{equation}
        x = [u, v, r, h, \dot{u}, \dot{v}, \dot{r}, \dot{h}]^T
    \end{equation}
    where $(u, v)$ are the center coordinates, $r$ is the aspect ratio, and $h$ is the height. This ensures the tracking stays smooth even during fast movements.

    \item DeepSORT "looks" at the object's actual appearance using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). It saves these features as a unique visual fingerprint.

    \item To match new detections with existing tracks, there are two scoring methods. First, the {Mahalanobis Distance} measures how far the detection is from the predicted position:
    \begin{equation}
        d^{(1)}(i, j) = (d_j - y_i)^T S_i^{-1} (d_j - y_i)
    \end{equation}
    Second, the {Cosine Similarity} checks if the visual fingerprint matches. These two scores are combined to ensure the match is accurate both spatially and visually.

    \item The {Hungarian Algorithm} make the final "decision" on which detection belongs to which ID. This ensures every object keeps a consistent unique ID from the moment it enters the view until it leaves.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Proposed Method} 
This study proposes a computer vision–based approach for detecting traffic signal violations using CCTV at Simpang Gedongan Sleman footage from a static camera. The system is designed as a multi-stage pipeline consisting of preprocessing, vehicle detection, object tracking, traffic light recognition, and violation analysis (Fig.~\ref{fig:proposed_method}). To improve efficiency and reduce noise, a polygon-based Region of Interest (ROI) is defined to limit the analysis to the roadway area, excluding irrelevant regions such as sidewalks and surrounding infrastructure.

Within this ROI, vehicle detection is performed using YOLOv8, focusing only on vehicles located inside the polygonal area. The detected vehicles are then processed using an object tracking method to assign unique identities and maintain their trajectories across consecutive frames. This tracking process enables consistent monitoring of each vehicle’s movement over time, which is essential for determining whether a vehicle has crossed a predefined boundary.

In parallel, the system identifies the traffic light state using a color-based approach, which is used as a condition in the violation detection process. A virtual stop line is defined within the ROI to represent the legal stopping boundary. A violation is detected when a tracked vehicle crosses this line while the traffic light is in a red state. The system then generates an output in the form of visual annotations and alert messages, providing a structured and automated method for traffic violation detection.

\subsection{Preprocessing}
Before being processed by the detection and tracking modules, the input frames undergo several preprocessing steps to improve efficiency and ensure consistency. First, each frame is resized to a fixed resolution of 768 × 768 pixels to match the input requirements of the detection model. This step also helps maintain a balance between computational cost and detection accuracy, particularly for detecting small objects such as motorcycles in CCTV footage.

Next, a Region of Interest (ROI) is defined in the form of a polygon to restrict the analysis to the roadway area. By applying this ROI, the system ignores irrelevant regions such as sidewalks, buildings, and other background elements, thereby reducing noise and improving detection performance. All subsequent processes, including vehicle detection and tracking, are performed only within this polygonal region.

\subsection{Vehicle Detection using YOLOv8}
The implementation using YOLOv8 follows a structured four stage pipeline:
\subsubsection{Data Input and Preprocessing}
The input for the system are from the Simpang Gedongan CCTV. To ensure computational efficiency and compatibility with the YOLOv8 architecture, the following preprocessing steps are performed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Frame Resizing:} Each input frame is spatially resized to a resolution of pixel. This resolution provides an optimal balance between inference speed and the ability to detect small objects.
    \item \textbf{Normalization:} Pixel intensities are normalized during the neural network's forward pass.
    \item \textbf{Polygonal ROI Masking:} To reduce false positives from non roadway areas, a custom polygonal Region of Interest (ROI) is defined. Only pixels within this mask are processed for vehicle detection.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Feature Extraction and Backbone}
The preprocessed frames are passed through the YOLOv8 Backbone. This stage extracts complex hierarchical features from the traffic scene. To ensure that both large trucks and small motorcycles are represented with high semantic richness.

\subsubsection{Anchor Free Prediction Head}
YOLOv8 implements an anchor-free detection paradigm. The model directly predicts the center point of the vehicle and the distances to the bounding box edges. This approach is particularly effective at Simpang Gedongan, where vehicles often overlap in congested traffic. 

\subsubsection{Post-Processing and Thresholding}
To refine the raw predictions, two primary filters are applied:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Confidence Thresholding:} Only detections with a confidence score $C > 0.5$ are retained, effectively filtering out low probability false detections.

    The system calculates a Confidence Score ($S_c$) based on the probability of an object's presence and the accuracy of the predicted bounding box ($B_p$) relative to the ground truth ($B_g$). The Intersection over Union (IoU) is defined as:

    \begin{equation}
        IoU = \frac{|B_p \cap B_g|}{|B_p \cup B_g|}
    \end{equation}

    The final confidence score for each detected vehicle is then formulated as:

    \begin{equation}
       S_c = P(\text{Object}) \times IoU_{pred}^{truth}
    \end{equation}

    where $P(\text{Object})$ represents the probability that the bounding box contains a vehicle, and $IoU_{pred}^{truth}$ measures the spatial overlap between the predicted box and the actual object location. Only detections satisfying $S_c > 0.5$ are passed to the tracking module.
    
    \item \textbf{Non Maximum Suppression (NMS):} To handle redundant detections of the same vehicle, NMS is applied with an Intersection over Union (IoU) threshold. This ensures each vehicle is represented by a single, accurate bounding box ($B_p$) before being passed to the DeepSORT tracking module.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Vehicle Tracking using DEEPSORT}

Deep SORT is utilized in this study as a robust multi-object tracking framework. By incorporating deep learning-based appearance features, Deep SORT exhibits good performance in managing object occlusions and maintaining tracking stability despite changes in vehicle scale or rotation.

The tracking mechanism primarily relies on the integration of two core algorithms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item{Kalman Filter:} 
This component is responsible for state estimation, predicting the future trajectory of detected vehicles in subsequent frames based on their current motion patterns.

    \item{Hungarian Algorithm:} To ensure data association, this algorithm utilizes a cost matrix to identify the optimal correspondence between the predicted positions from the Kalman Filter and the actual detections provided by YOLOv8.
\end{itemize}

In the context of this research, Deep SORT enables real-time monitoring of multiple vehicle trajectories. This continuous tracking is essential for the subsequent red-light violation analysis, as it allows the system to accurately determine if a specific vehicle ID has crossed the stop line during a prohibited signal state.

\subsection{Violation Detection}
The violation detection process is conducted by integrating vehicle trajectory information with the detected traffic light state (Fig.~\ref{fig:violation_detection}). A virtual boundary, referred to as the stop line, is defined within the Region of Interest (ROI) to represent the legal stopping limit at the intersection. Each detected vehicle is assigned a unique identity through the tracking module, enabling continuous monitoring of its movement across frames. By analyzing the position of each tracked vehicle relative to the stop line, the system determines whether the vehicle remains within the allowed area or crosses the predefined boundary.

At the same time, the traffic light state is identified using HSV-based color detection within a predefined traffic light region. A violation is defined as the event where a tracked vehicle crosses the stop line while the traffic light is in a red state. When this condition is met, the system flags the vehicle as a violator and generates visual outputs, such as bounding boxes, object IDs, and alert indicators, to clearly represent the detected violation.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
To assess the performance of the proposed traffic violation detection system, several standard statistical metrics will be utilized. These metrics provide a quantitative measure of how well the YOLOv8 and DEEPSORT model detects objects.
\subsubsection{Accuracy}
The ratio of total correct predictions to the total number of test cases.
$$Accuracy = \frac{TP + TN}{TP + TN + FP + FN}$$
\subsubsection{Precision}
Measures the proportion of predicted violations that were actually correct.
$$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}$$
\subsubsection{Recall}
Measures the system's ability to capture all actual violations.
$$Recall = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}$$
\subsubsection{F1-Score}
The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall.
$$F1 = 2 \times \frac{Precision \times Recall}{Precision + Recall}$$
